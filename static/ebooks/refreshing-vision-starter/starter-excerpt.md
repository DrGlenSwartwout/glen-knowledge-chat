## Try This: Four Read-and-Do Activities From the Book

Talking about Neuro-Fitness Training only goes so far. The heart of the book is a library of "read and do" activities like the four below, each with a stated purpose and simple instructions. None need any special equipment. Try one today.

### Palming

*State your purpose:* "I am developing awareness and control of the ability to rest and refresh my eyes, while increasing the ease of circulation for my eyes."

**The right stuff:** If desired, find something at chest height, while sitting, to rest your elbows on.

**Read and do**

1. Sit and warm up your hands by rubbing your palms vigorously together.
2. Place your softly cupped hands over your open eyes, with the heel of each hand resting on your cheekbone. The center of each palm is placed directly over each eye, without actually touching the eye.
3. To keep your neck and back straight with your shoulders relaxed, you can rest your elbows on a stack of books on your table or desk, or some other object at a comfortable chest height. The back of a chair placed in front of you may also work well.
4. Count 10 complete, relaxed breath cycles as you continue palming.
5. Try timing your palming sessions, and track how long your 10 breath cycles take to complete. The longer you take the better, as this means you are more relaxed and are breathing more fully.

### Eye Stretch

For eyestrain or headaches related to activities like studies, desk work or computer use, try stretching your eye muscles. Many people learn how to stretch the skeletal muscles for sports or yoga, but the eyes are surrounded by six muscles each, and they can get tired, tight and sore, too, contributing to fatigue and headaches.

Try doing this activity periodically to break up any long periods of intense visual stress such as studying for finals, or writing a paper. A college student who was having severe headaches from studying would even wake up in the morning with a headache still from the day before. As soon as he started using the eye stretch to break the muscle tension pattern in his eyes, he could not only manage the headaches, but also wake rested and refreshed for the first time at college.

When you are doing the activity notice when your breath spontaneously deepens and slows on an inhalation. This is a sign that your whole nervous system is letting go of its armoring around the stressful pattern of activity. The breath naturally seeks a coherent 10-second cycle of inhalation and exhalation when you are at peace. You may also notice a general relaxation response, releasing tension in the neck and shoulders, allowing the shoulders to broaden and drop. Your spine may straighten and elongate, and you may experience an expanded sense of interior spaciousness in your body space.

*State your purpose:* "I am developing freedom of movement in the connective tissues around my eyes."

**The right stuff:** No materials are needed.

**Read and do**

1. Stand in a relaxed posture, looking straight ahead with both eyes open.
2. Place the side of your index fingers against the skin to the right of each eye. Look to the left as you stretch the skin and connective tissues around your eyes to the right. Breathe in as you look to the left, and exhale as you look back to the center.
3. Now repeat this with your index fingers to the left of each eye, stretching to the left while looking to the right.
4. Next stretch downward with your fingers, contacting the skin just below your eyes, while looking upward.
5. Finally stretch the skin below your eyebrows upward with the sides of your index fingers.
6. Repeat this sequence 6 times, taking a longer, deeper breath each time as you are able to relax and stretch more.
7. Now try sustaining each stretch through one or more breath cycles. Work your way up to 6 cycles in each direction.

*Interesting question:* Can you visualize the oxygen from each breath circulating into the area you are stretching?

### Double Thumb Gate

*State your purpose:* "I am developing simultaneous awareness with my two eyes and connecting this with other senses."

**The right stuff:** No equipment needed.

**Read and do**

1. Standing comfortably with equal weight on both feet, and facing a blank wall (at first), hold your thumbs up at arms length in front of you about 4 inches apart. Look at the point on the wall that is directly between the two thumbs. Describe what you see, including the appearance of the thumbs. How many thumbs do you see? How does it change? Can you discover what you change internally that makes the appearance change?
2. Now begin to slowly move only the two arms and the eyes as a unit in various patterns, continuing to look directly between the location of the two thumbs to the wall. Move side to side, up and down, on both diagonals, in circles both directions, as well as random shapes. As you get better, try tracing the outline of objects in the room.
3. Wiggle one thumb and think about which image you feel the wiggle in. Now can you think the wiggle into the other image? Try this wiggling the other thumb. Now try wiggling both thumbs at the same time. Can you feel the wiggle in both right images, both left images, the middle two and the outer two?
4. Make a sound with one thumb by clicking the thumbnail against a fingernail of any finger on the same hand. Locate and move this sensation around to both of the thumb images like you did with the wiggle.

*Interesting question:* Do you ever see just 2 thumbs or 3? What happens, and which images look more real or more faded?

### Balance Sequence

*State your purpose:* "I am developing my body concept, balance and control while still and in motion."

**The right stuff:** No equipment needed.

**Read and do:** Practice each step until you can do it reasonably well, before going on to the next step.

1. Get on your hands and knees on the floor. Raise one hand in the air. When you can keep your balance, repeat, raising the other hand instead. Count to 10 while keeping your balance.
2. Raise one leg instead of a hand. Again count to 10 while keeping balance, first with one leg raised, and then with the other raised.
3. Now raise the hand and leg on the same side at the same time. Count to 10 while balancing. Repeat with the other side.
4. Now raise the arm and leg on opposite sides, counting to 10. Repeat with the other pair.
5. Balance on your tiptoes for a count of 10.
6. Rise smoothly and evenly from a sitting position on the floor while keeping your arms folded on your chest.
7. Keep your balance while walking forward and backward on your knees.
8. Stand on one foot for a count of ten. Then try the other foot.
9. Repeat steps 5 and 8 with your eyes closed.
10. Maintain your balance while jumping on one foot with your eyes closed. First use one foot and then the other.

The book's full sequence continues from here into rhythmic, two-limb variations, but this arc alone will already tell you a great deal about your own balance and body awareness.
