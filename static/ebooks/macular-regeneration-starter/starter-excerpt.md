## Macular Degeneration... Macular Regeneration

### What is the Macula?

The macula, or macula lutea ("yellow spot"), is the central part of the retina, responsible for our detailed vision. This is the part of the eye we look with, and is necessary for reading, writing, identifying faces and many other detailed visual tasks that most people take for granted. Slower reading with aging is one of the early signs of macular degeneration.

The incidence of macular degeneration increases from under 1% for people under age 50 to over a quarter of the population over age 75. In all, over 15 million Americans have macular degeneration, with 167,000 new cases added each year. It is the leading cause of irreversible blindness in this country. The two basic types are dry (85%) and wet (about 15%). Most cases progress slowly, while less than 10%, mostly of the wet type, progress rapidly. Some cases of wet macular degeneration are treatable by laser surgery, but the treatment itself cannot effectively seal up a leaking blood vessel without at the same time permanently destroying the retinal nerve fibers that pass through that area. This is why laser treatment can actually worsen vision, as reported by the National Eye Institute, and any ability to slow the progression of disease does not appear until at least a year after surgery. One surgeon states, "it is now increasingly evident that laser treatment will not significantly reduce the severe visual loss experienced by most patients with this macular disease.... Preventive measures, such as nutritional supplements and light filtration, should also be investigated in the hope of decelerating progressive, atrophic degeneration."

As always, prevention is the best medicine, when it is instituted in time and in adequate measure. Since less than 1% of those with macular degeneration have progressed to the point of legal blindness, most are in a position to greatly benefit from prevention.

### Fats and Oils

Thirty percent of the fat molecules in the brain and retina are docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), a long-chain essential fatty acid. In the photoreceptor cells specifically, DHA comprises 60% of the fats. DHA is made in the body from essential omega-3 fatty acids found in foods such as dark green leafy vegetables, walnuts, and flax seeds, but during degenerative processes the body cannot make adequate amounts, and dietary supplementation becomes important to help maintain or regenerate normal visual acuity. DHA is available pre-formed in fish oil, such as salmon oil, and in vegetarian DHA capsules derived from algae.

A low-fat diet may help reduce the risk of macular degeneration, with avoidance of red meat and dairy products. Avoidance of margarine and other hydrogenated (trans) oils is especially important in wet macular degeneration; it can take six months to a year to clear up their effects even after they are eliminated from the diet. Soft drusen are hydrophobic deposits of apparently undigestible fats, and increasing essential fatty acids while reducing trans, hydrogenated, rancid and oxidized fats in the diet may help reduce drusen formation, since essential fatty acids loosen the structure of fat-soluble cell membranes, while cholesterol, for example, increases rigidity.
